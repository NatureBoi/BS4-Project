Wave Propagation and Focal Mechanisms of Local Earthquakes in the Charlevoix Seismic Zone

Oluwaseun Idowu Fadugba, Charles Langston and Christine Powell

The Charlevoix seismic zone (CSZ) occurs along the ancient St. Lawrence rift zone in southeastern Quebec at the location of a Devonian impact crater. The crater is superimposed on major basement faults that range from Precambrian to Paleozoic in age and with a variety of dips. Previous stress inversions of local earthquake focal mechanisms in the CSZ suggest that the maximum principal horizontal stress is different than the first-order regional stress of the lithosphere due to ridge push. Significant local rotations of SHmax have also been reported. To explore whether these inferred stress rotations are real or an artifact of imprecise focal mechanisms, we perform a study of wave propagation for local earthquakes in the CSZ. Using multilayered velocity models inferred from earthquake tomography we compute synthetic seismograms to investigate the propagation of local P and S phases. An interesting observation is that the relative simplicity of the Charlevoix crust allows the creation of an SP head wave that propagates near the surface. Travel time of the SP head wave relative to S offers a sensitive constraint on source depth for events in the seismic zone, validating earthquake locations. Analysis of one well-recorded event (24 September 1996 earthquake with 3.1 MN) shows that near-surface structure can significantly distort apparent S wave polarization angles, causing errors in the source mechanism. The use of the absolute value of SV and SH amplitudes in addition to well characterized P and SH wave polarities is a useful expedient when modeling focal mechanisms with simple ray theory. In addition, a processing package is being developed to incorporate Green’s function amplitudes directly into mechanism inversions. Inclusion of SV and SH wave amplitude and polarity information significantly constrains local earthquake focal mechanisms for the relatively sparse seismic network around the CSZ. Although this is a work in progress, we expect to put realistic constraints on the variability of focal mechanisms within the CSZ to answer whether there are significant changes in maximum stress orientation within the zone.

Poster session presented at Next Generation Seismic Source Studies

2018 Fall Meeting of American Geophysical Union (AGU), Dec 10-14, 2018, Washington DC, USA.

Effects of pre-existing structures on the seismicity of the Charlevoix Seismic Zone.

Oluwaseun Fadugba, Eunseo Choi and Christine Powell

The Charlevoix seismic zone (CSZ) occurs along the ancient St. Lawrence rift zone in southeastern Quebec at the location of a major Devonian impact crater. The crater superimposed three major basement faults trending N35E and dipping at 70 degrees to the southeast. Many earthquakes are recorded each year in the CSZ and are concentrated within and beneath the impact crater. Some large-magnitude earthquakes associated with the rift faults occurred outside the crater. The primary goal of this research is to investigate combined effects of the pre-existing structures, the impact crater and the rift faults, and regional stresses on earthquake activity in the CSZ. Using PyLith, an open-source finite-element code for simulations of crustal deformation, we set up a model for a 150 x 150 x 40 km crustal block. The model includes the three rift faults with friction coefficient in the ranges of 0.1 and 0.6 and cohesion between several MPa to 10s of MPa. We will constrain the fault geometry using recent hypocenter relocation studies. The impact crater is represented by a spherical bowl with surface radius and depth of 30 and 15 km, respectively. Bulk and shear moduli for the region outside the crater are based on a regional velocity model for Quebec and the crater is modeled with reduced values: one-half and one-fourth the bulk and shear moduli and 0.9 of the density. Boundary displacements are applied in the direction of tectonic loading to increase differential stresses until the faults starts to slip, boundary displacements are incrementally applied on the side walls of the model domain in the direction of the region's greatest compressional principal stress. We will discuss whether modeled stress distributions can explain the regional seismicity as well as the recently relocated hypocenters. We also compare our results with those of Baird et al. (2010), who took similar approach to relate the regional structures, stresses and seismicity.

Poster session presented at The Eastern North American Margin: Structure, dynamics, history, and processes

2017 Fall Meeting of American Geophysical Union (AGU), Dec 11-15, 2017, New Orleans, LA USA.
